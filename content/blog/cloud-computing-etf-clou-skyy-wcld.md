# Cloud Computing ETFs 2026: CLOU vs SKYY vs WCLD — Recovery or Dead Money After the 58% Crash?

**By ETF Bridge Research** | July 11, 2026 | 9 min read

Few ETF categories have destroyed more wealth with a better narrative than cloud computing. In 2020-2021, cloud ETFs were the future — remote work, digital transformation, SaaS adoption, "software is eating the world." By November 2021, the Global X Cloud Computing ETF (CLOU) had assets of $1.5 billion and a share price near its all-time high. Then the Fed started hiking. By late 2022, CLOU had fallen 58%. WCLD — the WisdomTree Cloud Computing ETF, more concentrated in high-growth unprofitable SaaS — fell over 70%.

Now, in mid-2026, AI is driving a new wave of cloud infrastructure spending. Hyperscalers — Amazon AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud — are seeing revenue growth re-accelerate as enterprises rush to deploy AI workloads. The question: does AI lift all cloud boats, or only the infrastructure layer? And which cloud ETFs actually capture that spending?

**WCLD is the purest SaaS play** — no Microsoft, no Amazon, no Oracle. It holds high-growth software companies that primarily deliver their products via the cloud: Workday, ServiceNow, Salesforce, Atlassian, Datadog, CrowdStrike. The revenue-weighting methodology means faster-growing companies get higher weights. This is the highest-beta, highest-upside, highest-downside option.

**SKYY is the most diverse** — it holds cloud infrastructure (Amazon AWS, Microsoft Azure), enterprise software (Oracle, SAP, Adobe), networking (Arista Networks, Cisco), and some data center REITs. At 65 stocks with 0.60% fees, it's the broadest and most conservative cloud ETF — if that phrase isn't an oxymoron.

**CLOU sits in the middle** — SaaS-heavy but includes the major cloud platforms. The 0.68% fee is the highest of the group, which has been a persistent drag on returns.

**IGV is the stealth cloud ETF** — it's a broad software ETF, but software is overwhelmingly cloud-delivered in 2026. At 0.40% with $10B AUM and 120 holdings, it's the cheapest, most liquid, and arguably the best diversified way to play cloud computing. It just doesn't have "cloud" in the name.

## Why Cloud ETFs Crashed (And Why It Matters for the Recovery)

The 2021-2022 cloud crash wasn't a mystery — it was the predictable unwinding of a valuation bubble:

| Metric | Nov 2021 Peak | Dec 2022 Trough | Mid-2026 Current |
|---|---|---|---|
| WCLD Price | ~$65 | ~$18 (−72%) | ~$28 |
| CLOU Price | ~$30 | ~$12.50 (−58%) | ~$20 |
| SKYY Price | ~$110 | ~$60 (−45%) | ~$92 |
| IGV Price | ~$450 | ~$280 (−38%) | ~$448 |
| Cloud company median P/S | ~25x | ~6x | ~10x |
| Cloud company median P/E | Negative (unprofitable) | N/A | ~35x (for profitable ones) |

The crash had three drivers, and only one has been resolved:

1. **Valuation**: Cloud stocks traded at 15-30x revenue in 2021. These are not chip companies with 60% gross margins and massive operating leverage — cloud software companies typically have 70-80% gross margins but high sales and marketing costs that keep many of them unprofitable on a GAAP basis. The 25x revenue multiple was never sustainable.

2. **Rising rates**: Cloud stocks are long-duration assets — their value depends on future cash flows discounted to the present. When the Fed raised rates from 0% to 5.25% in 18 months, the present value of those future cash flows collapsed. This was mechanical, not fundamental.

3. **Demand normalization**: The 2020-2021 pull-forward of cloud adoption (remote work, digital everything) created a demand hangover in 2022-2023. Companies had overbought cloud software and needed time to digest. Growth rates decelerated across the sector.

**What's changed for 2026**: Rates are lower (Fed has cut to ~3.75%). Valuations have reset from 25x to 10x revenue — still not cheap, but no longer absurd. And critically, AI is creating a new demand driver: enterprises need cloud infrastructure to deploy AI models, and they need modern SaaS platforms that integrate AI features. The demand normalization has ended. Growth is re-accelerating.

The 5-year numbers are grim. WCLD has **lost** 10% annualized over five years — a $10,000 investment in January 2021 is worth roughly $5,900 today. CLOU is essentially flat over five years. SKYY has compounded at +4%, half the S&P 500's return. Only IGV, with its broader software exposure, has come close to matching the market.

The 2025-2026 AI-driven recovery is real but insufficient to compensate for the depth of the 2021-2022 losses. Cloud ETFs remain **deeply underwater** for anyone who bought before 2023.

## Which Cloud ETF Makes Sense?

| Cloud ETF | Best For | Key Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **IGV (0.40%)** | Core software/cloud exposure; broadest, cheapest, most liquid. It's not called "cloud" but it functionally is. | Less pure-play; might miss smaller high-growth SaaS names. |
| **SKYY (0.60%)** | Balanced cloud infrastructure + software; most defensive. | Higher fee; conservative weights mean less upside if pure SaaS rallies. |
| **CLOU (0.68%)** | Broad cloud pure-play; reasonable middle ground. | Highest fee; historically poor risk-adjusted returns. |
| **WCLD (0.45%)** | Aggressive, high-conviction SaaS bet; highest upside if growth/rate environment stays favorable. | Still 56% below its 2021 peak. Another rate shock could push it to new lows. |

**The practical answer**: For most investors, IGV does the job at the lowest cost. It captures the software/cloud theme with diversification and liquidity. If you specifically want unprofitable, high-growth SaaS exposure — the kind of companies that could 5x if AI adoption accelerates — WCLD is the vehicle, but only as a small (2-3%) satellite position. It is not a core holding. It is a bet.

## Portfolio Sizing Rules for Cloud ETFs

If you already hold VOO or QQQ, you have significant cloud exposure:

| Stock | S&P 500 Weight | Cloud Business |
|---|---|---|
| Microsoft (Azure) | ~6.5% | Largest cloud platform after AWS |
| Amazon (AWS) | ~4% | Largest cloud platform |
| Alphabet (Google Cloud) | ~4% | Third-largest cloud platform |
| Oracle (OCI) | ~1.5% | Growing cloud infrastructure |
| Salesforce | ~0.5% | Largest SaaS company |

**You already own roughly 15-20% exposure to cloud computing through the major indices.** Adding a dedicated cloud ETF on top is a double-down, not a diversification. Size it accordingly — no more than 5-8% of total equity as a satellite.

## The Bottom Line

Cloud computing ETFs are the poster child for narrative-driven investing gone wrong. The thesis — software is eating the world, everything is moving to the cloud — was correct. The timing and valuation of the ETFs that captured it were catastrophic. WCLD investors who bought in 2021 are still down 56% despite the AI recovery.

The lesson: a correct secular thesis is not enough. Valuation, timing, and fee structure matter enormously. If you want cloud exposure in 2026, IGV (0.40%) is the most sensible vehicle — broad software, reasonable fees, liquid, not dependent on unprofitable SaaS companies delivering on aggressive growth targets. If you want a high-conviction bet on AI-driven SaaS acceleration, WCLD is the purest expression — but keep it sized as a bet, not a conviction position disguised as diversification.
