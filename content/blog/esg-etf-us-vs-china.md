# ESG ETFs 2026: US vs China — Same Label, Different Portfolios

**By ETF Bridge Research** | July 5, 2026 | 9 min read

ESG is the most controversial three-letter acronym in investing. To its advocates, it's the framework for aligning capital with values — reducing carbon emissions, improving corporate governance, and funding the transition to a sustainable economy. To its critics, it's a marketing label that charges higher fees for portfolios that aren't meaningfully different from the broad market.

The truth is somewhere in between — and it depends entirely on **which market's ESG ETF you're buying**.

A US ESG ETF (like ESGU) looks a lot like the S&P 500 with the worst governance offenders excluded. A China ESG ETF doesn't look like the CSI 300 at all — it's heavily concentrated in solar, batteries, EVs, and grid infrastructure. Same label. Radically different portfolios. This article compares the two approaches.

## The US Approach: Exclusion + Tilting

US ESG ETFs primarily work through **negative screening** — exclude companies in controversial industries (tobacco, weapons, thermal coal) — and **ESG score tilting** — overweight companies with high MSCI ESG ratings.

### Major US ESG ETFs

| ETF | Ticker | Expense Ratio | AUM | Methodology |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **iShares ESG Aware MSCI USA** | ESGU | 0.15% | ~$25B | Tilts toward high ESG scores; excludes worst offenders |
| **iShares MSCI USA ESG Select** | SUSA | 0.25% | ~$5B | Selects top ESG performers in each sector |
| **Vanguard ESG US Stock** | ESGV | 0.09% | ~$9B | Excludes adult entertainment, alcohol, tobacco, weapons, fossil fuels, gambling, nuclear |
| **SPDR S&P 500 ESG** | EFIV | 0.10% | ~$2B | S&P 500 minus companies not meeting S&P's ESG criteria |

Critically, US ESG ETFs still look **very similar to the broad market**:

| | ESGU (ESG) | VOO (S&P 500) | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Top holding** | Nvidia (~8%) | Nvidia (~8%) | Same |
| **Technology weight** | ~37% | ~39% | Nearly identical |
| **Energy weight** | ~1% | ~3% | ESG underweights fossil fuels |
| **Number of holdings** | ~290 | ~503 | ESG holds fewer |

ESGU excludes roughly 200 of the S&P 500's names — mostly on governance, environmental, or controversial business grounds — but the overlap with VOO is roughly 85% by weight. You're paying 0.15% instead of 0.03% for a portfolio that is, in most dimensions, the market portfolio minus fossil fuels and defense contractors.

## The China Approach: The State-Directed Green Economy

China doesn't have a Western-style ESG framework built around shareholder activism and corporate governance scores. Instead, "ESG" in China is synonymous with **the government's green transition policies** — primarily the "dual carbon" targets (peak emissions by 2030, carbon neutrality by 2060).

Chinese "ESG" ETFs overwhelmingly hold:
- Solar manufacturers (LONGi, Tongwei, Jinko)
- Battery makers (CATL, BYD)
- EV manufacturers (BYD, NIO, Xpeng)
- Grid equipment (State Grid suppliers)
- New energy infrastructure

The contrast is stark: a China ESG ETF is, in practice, a **new energy sector bet**. It's not about excluding bad actors or tilting toward better-governed companies. It's about investing in the industries the Chinese government has designated as strategic priorities.

## Head-to-Head: US vs China ESG

| | US ESG (ESGU) | China Green/ESG (516160) |
|---|---|---|
| **Philosophy** | Exclude bad actors; tilt toward good governance | Invest in the green transition |
| **Top sector** | Technology (37%) | New Energy equipment & materials |
| **Fossil fuel exposure** | ~1% (minimal) | ~0% |
| **Looks like the broad market?** | Yes — ~85% overlap with S&P 500 | No — entirely different from CSI 300 |
| **Expense ratio** | 0.09–0.25% | 0.15–0.59% |
| **Returns driven by** | Broad market + ESG tilt (small) | Government policy + energy sector cycle |
| **Governance focus** | High — board independence, shareholder rights | Low — state ownership is the norm |

**The fundamental insight**: A US ESG ETF is the market minus the worst offenders. A China ESG ETF is a sector bet on the energy transition. If you buy both expecting the same thing, you don't understand what you own.

## Key Takeaways

1. **US ESG ETFs are broad-market-light.** They exclude sin stocks and fossil fuels but remain ~85% correlated with the S&P 500. The ESG "premium" is small — both in fees and in portfolio differentiation.

2. **China ESG ETFs are green sector bets.** They're concentrated in solar, batteries, EVs, and grid — not diversified core holdings. They move with energy policy and commodity cycles, not broad equity markets.

3. **Know what you're buying.** ESG means fundamentally different things in different markets. Read the top 10 holdings before assuming an ESG label tells you anything about the portfolio.

4. **Cost matters even for values-based investing.** ESGV at 0.09% is the cheapest US ESG option. China's 0.20% onshore green ETFs are competitively priced.
