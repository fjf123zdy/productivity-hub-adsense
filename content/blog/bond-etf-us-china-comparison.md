# Bond ETFs for Equity Investors: US Treasuries vs China Sovereign — A 2026 Guide

**By ETF Bridge Research** | July 1, 2026 | 9 min read

---

This series has spent fourteen articles analyzing stock ETFs. But every equity portfolio has a silent partner: the bonds you hold (or don't hold) to stabilize returns when stocks fall.

Bond ETFs are the simplest way for equity investors to add fixed-income exposure — one trade, diversified holdings, professional management. But the world of bonds is far less intuitive than stocks. You need to understand duration, credit quality, yield curves, and currency exposure. And in 2026, with US rates elevated and Chinese bonds offering surprising diversification benefits, the US-vs-China question extends to fixed income.

This article is a bond ETF primer for equity investors, focused on the two sovereign markets most relevant to our readers.

---

## Why Equity Investors Hold Bond ETFs

| Role in Portfolio | How Bonds Help | Which Bond ETF |
|---|---|---|
| **Volatility dampener** | Bonds tend to rise when stocks crash (flight to safety) | Short-to-intermediate US Treasuries (BND, SHY) |
| **Income generator** | Bonds pay predictable coupons — useful in retirement | Longer-duration corporates or sovereigns |
| **Rebalancing dry powder** | When stocks fall, sell bonds to buy cheap equities | Short-duration, highly liquid Treasury ETFs |
| **Inflation hedge** | TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities) adjust with CPI | TIP (US TIPS ETF) |

> **The 60/40 portfolio** — 60% stocks, 40% bonds — was the standard for decades. It fell out of favor after bonds and stocks crashed together in 2022. But with yields now materially higher than the near-zero rates of 2020–2021, bonds are once again earning their place as diversifiers.

## Bond ETF Basics: Three Things Equity Investors Need to Understand

### 1. Duration

Duration measures sensitivity to interest rate changes. It's the bond equivalent of beta:

| Duration | What It Means | Example ETF |
|---|---|---|
| **Short (0–3 years)** | Low sensitivity. If rates rise 1%, the ETF falls ~1–3% | SHY (1–3 Year Treasury, ~1.9yr duration) |
| **Intermediate (3–10 years)** | Moderate sensitivity. 1% rate rise → ~4–7% price drop | BND (Total Bond Market, ~6yr duration) |
| **Long (10–30 years)** | High sensitivity. 1% rate rise → ~15–20% price drop | TLT (20+ Year Treasury, ~16yr duration) |

A long-duration bond ETF like TLT can gain or lose 15–20% in a year — volatility that rivals stocks. Equity investors who want true diversification should generally stick to **short-to-intermediate** duration where price swings are manageable.

### 2. Credit Quality

| Credit Rating | What It Means | Default Risk | Yield Premium Over Treasuries |
|---|---|---|---|
| **AAA / US Treasury** | Backed by the full faith of the US government | Effectively zero | — (the risk-free rate) |
| **AA / A (Investment Grade)** | High-quality corporate or sovereign | Very low (~0.1% annual default rate) | +0.5–1.5% |
| **BBB (Lowest Investment Grade)** | Still investment grade, but watch the credit cycle | Low (~0.3% default) | +1.0–2.0% |
| **BB and below (High Yield / Junk)** | Speculative | Material (~3–5% annual default) | +3.0–6.0% |

Equity investors using bonds as portfolio stabilizers should stay in **US Treasuries or investment-grade sovereigns**. High-yield bonds correlate too closely with stocks to provide diversification.

### 3. Yield-to-Maturity (YTM)

The single most important number on a bond ETF's fact sheet. It tells you the annualized return you'd get if you held every bond in the portfolio to maturity, assuming no defaults. **YTM is a better predictor of future returns than past performance.**

| Bond ETF | Approximate YTM (mid-2026) |
|---|---|
| SHY (1–3yr Treasury) | ~4.5% |
| BND (Total US Bond Market) | ~5.0% |
| TLT (20+yr Treasury) | ~5.2% |
| China Sovereign Bond ETF | ~2.5–3.5% |

### BND — The Default Bond ETF

BND holds over 10,000 US government and investment-grade corporate bonds. It's the bond market's equivalent of VOO — broad, cheap (0.03%), and good enough for most investors. If you add one bond ETF, this is it.

| Metric | BND |
|---|---|
| **AUM** | ~$350 billion |
| **Holdings** | 10,000+ bonds |
| **Avg Duration** | ~6.0 years |
| **SEC Yield** | ~5.0% |
| **2025 Return** | ~+7% |
| **2026 YTD** | ~+4% |
| **2022 Return** | −13% (worst year ever — rates spiked) |

The 2022 drawdown was historically extreme (the worst bond bear market in 250 years of data). With yields now at 5% rather than 1%, forward returns are far more attractive — and the downside from further rate hikes is limited compared to the zero-rate era.

## China Sovereign Bond ETFs: The Diversification Opportunity

Chinese government bonds have been one of the best-performing bond markets globally, driven by falling domestic rates (PBOC easing) and a stable credit profile:

| Metric | China Sovereign Bond ETF |
|---|---|
| **Index** | Bloomberg China Treasury + Policy Bank |
| **Yield** | ~2.5–3.0% (lower than US nominally) |
| **Duration** | ~4–5 years (intermediate) |
| **Credit Rating** | A+ (S&P) / A1 (Moody's) — investment grade |
| **2025 Return** | **+11.6%** (unhedged) / **+17.5%** (USD-hedged) |
| **2026 YTD** | **+8.0%** (leading all bond ETF categories) |
| **Currency Risk** | CNY depreciation can wipe out gains for unhedged holders |

### Why Chinese Bonds Have Outperformed

China's central bank (PBOC) has been cutting rates to support the economy, while the Fed has kept rates elevated to fight inflation. Falling Chinese rates → rising Chinese bond prices → strong capital gains for bond ETF holders. This is the mirror image of what happened to US bonds in 2022 (rising rates → falling prices).

The outperformance is a rate-cycle story, not a credit story. If the Fed cuts aggressively and the PBOC pauses, US Treasuries will outperform. The direction depends on central bank policy, not the inherent quality of the bonds.

### The Currency Problem

For non-CNY investors, Chinese bond returns look great in local currency (+11.6% in 2025). But the CNY weakened against the USD for much of 2022–2025, which would have significantly reduced returns for dollar-based investors:

| Share Class | 2025 Return (China Sovereign Bond ETF) |
|---|---|
| **Unhedged (in CNY)** | +11.6% |
| **USD-Hedged** | **+17.5%** |

The hedged version captured falling Chinese rates *plus* the positive carry from the US-China interest rate differential. The unhedged version got the bond rally but lost on currency. **For bond ETFs, hedging matters even more than for equity ETFs** — because bond returns are smaller, and currency swings can dominate them.

## How to Add Bonds to an Equity Portfolio

### Option 1: The Simple Approach

**Add 20–40% BND** (or equivalent total bond market ETF) to your equity portfolio. Rebalance annually. Accept that bonds will sometimes lose money (2022), but will provide income and crisis protection most of the time.

### Option 2: The Duration-Matched Approach

Match your bond duration to your time horizon:

| Time Horizon | Bond ETF | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **Cash / <2 years** | SHY (1–3yr Treasury) | 1.9 years |
| **2–5 years** | BND (Total Bond) | 6 years |
| **5–10 years** | IEF (7–10yr Treasury) | 7 years |
| **10+ years / pension fund** | TLT (20+yr Treasury) | 16 years |

### Option 3: The Cross-Border Approach

**Hold both US and China sovereign bonds** — roughly 70/30 US/China — for genuine diversification. The correlation between US Treasury yields and Chinese government bond yields is low (~0.3–0.4), meaning they often move in different directions, providing a smoother total return.

## Key Takeaways

1. **BND (0.03%) is the VOO of bonds** — if you add one fixed-income ETF to an equity portfolio, this is it.

2. **Duration is your risk knob.** Short-duration for stability (SHY, ~4.5% yield, low volatility). Long-duration for speculation or deflation hedging (TLT, ~5.2% yield, equity-like volatility).

3. **Chinese sovereign bonds have been a top-performing asset class** — +11.6% in 2025, +8% YTD 2026, driven by PBOC rate cuts. But the currency issue is real: unhedged returns can be much lower.

4. **Hedge your bond ETFs, not your stock ETFs.** Bond returns are small enough that currency swings can dominate. Equity returns are large enough to absorb FX noise over time.

5. **The diversification benefit is back.** With bond yields at 5% rather than 0%, bonds once again provide meaningful income and portfolio stabilization. The 2022 bond crash was an anomaly driven by the zero-rate starting point — not a permanent condition.

6. **Start simple.** BND or SHY. Add Chinese sovereign exposure only if you already have a diversified core and want incremental diversification.
