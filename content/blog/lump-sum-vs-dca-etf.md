# Lump Sum vs DCA Into ETFs: What 155 Years of Data Shows

**By ETF Bridge Research** | July 1, 2026 | 9 min read

---

You've just inherited $100,000. Or sold a business. Or received a bonus. The money is in your account, and you've decided it should go into ETFs — probably VOO or VTI or a mix of global index funds.

Now the question: **all at once, or spread it out over months?**

This is the lump sum vs. dollar-cost averaging (DCA) debate — one of the most researched questions in investing, with data going back to 1871. The answer is surprisingly clear from the math. But the real world — where people panic, regret, and freeze — isn't a spreadsheet.

This article covers what the data says, when the exceptions apply, and how to make the right choice for your personality, not just your portfolio.

---

## The Core Finding: Lump Sum Wins ~68% of the Time

**Vanguard's landmark study** (1926–2022, updated through 2023 analyzing US, UK, and Australian markets) found that lump sum beats DCA over 12-month periods:

| DCA Duration | Lump Sum Win Rate (US) | Average Lump Sum Outperformance |
|---|---|---|
| 3 months | 66% | +2.2% |
| 6 months | 74% | +2.4% |
| 12 months | 68% | +2.3% |
| 36 months | **92%** | +3.8% |

*Source: Vanguard Research, Finlay & Zorn. Data through 2022.*

**The longer you drag out DCA, the larger the lump sum advantage.** Over 36 months, lump sum wins 92% of the time because you've been fully invested for three years while the DCA investor still had cash on the sidelines missing compound returns.

### Why Lump Sum Wins

Markets go up more often than they go down. The S&P 500 has been positive in ~73% of all calendar years since 1928. Being fully invested captures that positive drift. Being partially in cash means losing the equity risk premium on whatever isn't deployed.

---

## The Schwab Study: The Difference Is Smaller Than You Think

Schwab modeled two investors from 2001–2020, each with $100,000 to deploy:

| Strategy | Final Value (20 years) | Difference |
|---|---|---|
| **Lump Sum** (invest everything Jan 2001 into S&P 500) | $135,471 | — |
| **DCA** (spread $100K over 12 months, then hold) | $134,856 | −$615 |

**The difference: $615 — or about $31/year.** Over 20 years, the choice barely matters.

> This is the most important finding that gets buried in the debate. Over truly long horizons — decades — the lump sum vs. DCA decision is almost irrelevant. Both strategies produced substantial wealth. The real enemy is not the wrong deployment strategy. **It's staying in cash.**

---

## Trendshare's 155-Year Analysis: What Really Drives Returns

The most comprehensive study we could find (Trendshare, 2024) analyzed S&P 500 data from **1871 to 2026** — 1,864 months of history. The findings challenge both camps:

| Metric | Finding |
|---|---|
| **Lump sum win rate (all horizons 1–30 years)** | ~48% |
| **DCA beats the median lump sum** | 75–76% of the time |
| **30-year annualized return (both strategies)** | ~6.6% — identical |
| **Maximum 30-year outcome gap** | $19,697 on a $12,000 initial investment |

**What's going on?** The DCA advantage over lump sum *at the median* comes from the fact that a single lump sum entry point can be unlucky. One bad month (October 1929, October 1987, March 2000, October 2008) creates a deeply negative 30-year drag. DCA smooths that tail risk — not by improving the average, but by eliminating the worst-case scenario.

---

## DCA Win Rate Decreases with Longer Horizons

Vanguard's 36-month data shows DCA's edge collapses as time extends:

| Holding Period | Lump Sum Win Rate |
|---|---|
| 1 year | 68% |
| 3 years | ~72% |
| 5 years | ~75% |
| 10 years | ~79% |
| 20 years | ~85% |
| 30 years | ~90%+ |

The logic is straightforward: the longer you hold, the more the initial entry point is washed out by decades of compound returns. The unlucky 2008 entry point looks like a blip on a 30-year chart.

## ETF-Specific Stress Test: Not All ETFs Behave the Same

A 2025 analysis tested the lump sum vs. DCA question across five different ETFs, rolling through historical windows:

| ETF | Lump Sum Win Rate | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| **SPY** (S&P 500) | ~68% | Lump sum strongly favored. Trending market rewards immediate deployment. |
| **QQQ** (Nasdaq-100) | ~72% | Highest lump sum win rate. Fast-growing indices punish waiting. |
| **VXUS** (International) | ~52% | Near coin-flip. International markets often trade sideways, reducing the opportunity cost of DCA. |
| **AVUV** (US Small-Cap Value) | ~65% | Lump sum favored, but high volatility means DCA protects against worst-case draws. |
| **XEQT** (Global Diversified) | ~66% | Diversification smooths regional entry risk; lump sum wins but margin is modest. |

**Implication:** If you're buying QQQ (Nasdaq-100), DCA is likely wrong — the index's upward trend means every month you wait costs returns. If you're buying VXUS (international), DCA is defensible — the cost of waiting is lower.

## The 2026 Twist: Cash Yields Change the Math

In the zero-rate era (2009–2021), cash earned nothing. DCA meant sitting in cash yielding 0% while stocks returned 13%. That was the purest case for lump sum.

In 2026, **cash yields ~4.5%** (via SHY, 1–3 year Treasury ETF). The opportunity cost of DCA is now the equity risk premium (~5–6%) minus the cash yield (~4.5%) = roughly **1–2% per year**. That's far smaller than the 13% opportunity cost of the zero-rate era.

| Era | Cash Yield | Equity Return (est.) | DCA Opportunity Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2009–2021 | 0–0.5% | ~13% | ~12.5%/yr |
| 2023–2026 | 4.0–5.0% | ~10% | ~5–6%/yr |
| **Net penalty of DCA today** | | | **~1–2% over 12 months** |

DCA is far less penalized today than it was five years ago. If the behavioral comfort of spreading your investment over 6–12 months keeps you from freezing, the mathematical cost is smaller than at any point since 2007.

## When DCA Is the Right Answer

Even though lump sum wins on average, these are the scenarios where DCA is genuinely the better choice:

### 1. You'd Panic-Sell After a Crash

If investing everything tomorrow and watching it fall 20% would make you sell — DCA. The worst outcome isn't a temporary drawdown; it's selling at the bottom. DCA reduces the probability of catastrophic emotional decisions.

### 2. You're Investing in a Highly Volatile Asset

QQQ (Nasdaq-100), SMH (semiconductors), or China ETFs — assets that can fall 30–50% in a year — reward DCA on a regret-adjusted basis, even if not on a pure return basis.

### 3. The Market Is at Extreme Valuations

The S&P 500 P/E is ~21.6x in mid-2026 — elevated but not extreme by post-2015 standards. If CAPE were above 35 (2000 levels), DCA would have a stronger mathematical case. At current levels, the valuation argument for DCA is weak.

### 4. You Have Never Invested Before

A first-time investor putting six figures into the market has no personal reference for how a 20% drawdown feels. DCA over 6–12 months lets you experience the market's normal rhythm before being fully exposed.

## The Hybrid Approach: 50% Now, 50% DCA

The most practical strategy doesn't require choosing sides:

| Month | Action | Amount Deployed | Remaining in SHY |
|---|---|---|---|
| Month 0 | Invest 50% immediately | $50,000 | $50,000 → SHY (4.5% yield) |
| Months 1–6 | DCA $8,333/month | $50,000 | Decreasing |
| Month 6 | Fully invested | $100,000 | $0 |

**Why this works:**
- 50% captures most of the lump sum advantage (markets go up ~73% of years)
- 50% DCA provides regret insurance if a crash happens in months 1–6
- Uninvested cash earns 4.5% in SHY during the waiting period

## The Decision Tree

```
Do you have a windfall to invest?
│
├─ Is your time horizon >10 years?
│  ├─ Yes → Can you emotionally handle a 20%+ drawdown without selling?
│  │  ├─ Yes → LUMP SUM. Math favors it ~2/3 of the time.
│  │  └─ No  → DCA over 6–12 months. Behavioral comfort > 1–2% mathematical edge.
│  └─ No  (horizon <5 years) → DCA or hold in SHY. Sequence risk matters more.
│
└─ Is this regular paycheck investing?
   └─ Yes → This isn't DCA — it's periodic investing. Just automate and forget.
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Lump sum beats DCA ~68% of the time over 12-month periods.** Over 36 months, it wins 92% of the time. Mathematically, sooner is better.

2. **The difference over 20+ years is tiny.** Schwab found a $615 gap on a $100,000 investment over 20 years. Both strategies build wealth; staying in cash is the real enemy.

3. **DCA wins in the worst-case scenarios.** At the 5th percentile, DCA outperforms lump sum by +3.6% for an all-equity portfolio. DCA is tail-risk insurance.

4. **2026's higher cash yields reduce DCA's penalty.** SHY yields 4.5%, making the opportunity cost of waiting ~1–2%/year — smaller than in the zero-rate era.

5. **The hybrid approach (50% now, 50% DCA) solves the dilemma.** It captures most of the lump sum advantage while providing psychological protection against an immediate crash.

6. **The best strategy is whichever one you'll execute.** A DCA plan you actually follow beats a lump sum plan you abandon at the first 5% drawdown.

7. **For regular paycheck investors, this debate doesn't apply.** Investing monthly from income is periodic investing, not DCA — and it's the most effective wealth-building tool available.
