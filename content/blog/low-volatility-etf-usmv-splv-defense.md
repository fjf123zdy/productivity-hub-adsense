# Low-Volatility ETFs 2026: USMV vs SPLV — Defense When Growth Cracks

**By ETF Bridge Research** | July 13, 2026 | 8 min read

The most reliable pattern in equity markets is not "stocks go up over time" — it's "stocks go down, sometimes violently, and investors panic-sell at exactly the wrong moment."

In 2022, the S&P 500 fell 19% from peak to trough. In February-March 2020, it fell 34% in 33 days. In 2008, 57%. In 2000-2002, 49%. The human response — sell, wait for clarity, buy back when things feel safe — is the single largest destroyer of long-term returns. Dalbar's annual investor behavior study consistently finds that the average investor underperforms the funds they own by 2-3% annually, almost entirely due to bad timing decisions during volatile periods.

Low-volatility ETFs are designed to solve this problem — not by eliminating volatility (impossible), but by reducing it enough that you can actually hold on during the worst drawdowns. This article compares the two dominant low-vol strategies — USMV (minimum volatility) and SPLV (low volatility selection) — and explains why a permanent defensive allocation may be the most important portfolio decision you make.

## The Two Low-Volatility Strategies

| ETF | Ticker | Expense Ratio | AUM | Holdings | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **iShares MSCI USA Min Vol Factor** | USMV | 0.15% | ~$30B | ~160 stocks | Optimizes for minimum portfolio volatility; sector-constrained |
| **Invesco S&P 500 Low Volatility** | SPLV | 0.25% | ~$10B | ~100 stocks | Selects the 100 least volatile S&P 500 stocks; equal-weight |

Despite the similar name ("low volatility"), these two ETFs use fundamentally different methodologies that produce meaningfully different portfolios.

### USMV: Optimization, Not Selection

USMV doesn't simply pick the least volatile stocks. It runs a mathematical optimization that constructs the **minimum possible volatility portfolio** from roughly 600 US large and mid-cap stocks, subject to constraints (no stock above 1.5%, sector weights constrained to ±5% of the parent index, turnover limits). The result is a portfolio designed to be less volatile than the broad market as a whole, not just a collection of whatever stocks happen to be the least volatile right now.

The optimization approach means USMV holds some stocks you wouldn't expect. A low-volatility utility stock might be excluded if it's highly correlated with other utilities in the portfolio — the optimizer cares about **portfolio-level volatility**, not stock-level volatility. Diversification matters. Two moderately volatile stocks with low correlation can reduce portfolio volatility more than one very low-volatility stock.

### SPLV: Simple Selection

SPLV takes a simpler approach: rank all S&P 500 stocks by trailing 12-month realized volatility, select the 100 least volatile, weight them equally, and rebalance quarterly. No optimization. No correlation modeling. Just the 100 most boring stocks, equal weight.

The simplicity has advantages — SPLV is transparent, predictable, and easy to understand. The disadvantages: equal weighting means a $5 billion low-volatility stock gets the same weight as a $500 billion low-volatility stock, which skews the portfolio toward mid-caps; and the quarterly rebalance means the portfolio can rotate sharply when volatility regimes shift.

## Portfolio Comparison

| Dimension | USMV | SPLV |
|---|---|---|
| **Top Sector** | Technology (~18%) | Utilities (~25%) |
| **Second Sector** | Healthcare (~17%) | Consumer Staples (~20%) |
| **Third Sector** | Consumer Staples (~12%) | Healthcare (~15%) |
| **Mag7 Exposure** | ~12% (Microsoft, Alphabet mostly) | ~3% (effectively none) |
| **Median Market Cap** | ~$80B | ~$25B |
| **Yield** | ~1.8% | ~2.2% |
| **P/E Ratio** | ~19x | ~17x |
| **Beta (to S&P 500)** | ~0.75 | ~0.65 |

The key difference: **USMV is a technology-light, healthcare-heavy version of the US market. SPLV is a utilities-and-staples fund with an S&P 500 wrapper.**

USMV's technology allocation (roughly 18%) is the most important number in the table. It means USMV holds some tech — Microsoft, Alphabet, Accenture, Cisco — but in much lower weights than the S&P 500 (32% tech). SPLV holds almost no tech at all — the 100 least volatile S&P 500 stocks are overwhelmingly utilities, consumer staples, and healthcare names. In a tech-driven bull market, SPLV will dramatically underperform. In a tech-driven selloff, SPLV will dramatically outperform.

## The Bear Market Test

| Period | USMV | SPLV | VOO (S&P 500) | USMV Outperformance | SPLV Outperformance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2022 Full Year | −8% | −5% | −19% | **+11%** | **+14%** |
| Feb-Mar 2020 (COVID crash) | −22% | −28% | −34% | **+12%** | **+6%** |
| Q4 2018 (Fed hike scare) | −11% | −9% | −14% | +3% | +5% |
| 2008 GFC (simulated) | −35% est. | −30% est. | −57% | +22% | +27% |

In every major drawdown of the last 15 years, both USMV and SPLV lost less than the S&P 500 — anywhere from 3% to 27% less. The protection isn't perfect (these are still equity ETFs; they will lose money in bear markets), but it's meaningful. Cutting a 19% loss to 8% or 5% is the difference between staying invested and panic-selling.

**The COVID anomaly**: SPLV fell more than USMV in the COVID crash (−28% vs −22%) because the selloff was indiscriminate — everything crashed together, and SPLV's equal-weight mid-cap exposure amplified the drawdown relative to USMV's large-cap optimization. The lesson: in a liquidity-driven crash where correlations go to 1, minimum volatility optimization provides better protection than simple low-volatility selection.

## The Bull Market Cost

Low-volatility strategies protect in down markets. The cost: they underperform in up markets. There is no free lunch.

| Period | USMV | SPLV | VOO (S&P 500) | USMV Underperformance | SPLV Underperformance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2025 | +15% | +8% | +25% | −10% | −17% |
| 2024 | +14% | +10% | +26% | −12% | −16% |
| 2023 | +12% | +4% | +26% | −14% | −22% |
| 2021 | +22% | +18% | +29% | −7% | −11% |
| YTD 2026 | +9% | +7% | +11% | −2% | −4% |

In 2023-2024 — two of the best years for growth stocks in decades — SPLV dramatically underperformed, returning only 4% and 10% vs the S&P 500's 26% in both years. USMV's optimization allowed it to capture more upside while still protecting on the downside.

**The asymmetry**: USMV captures roughly 75-80% of the S&P 500's upside and only 50-60% of its downside. Over a full market cycle, this asymmetry can produce risk-adjusted returns that match or exceed the market — even though USMV underperforms in pure bull markets. SPLV captures roughly 60% of the upside and 40% of the downside — more protection, but at a higher long-term cost.

| Metric | USMV | SPLV | VOO |
|---|---|---|---|
| 10-Year Ann. Return | +11.5% | +10.2% | +14.0% |
| 10-Year Volatility | 12.5% | 11.0% | 15.5% |
| Sharpe Ratio | 0.84 | 0.83 | 0.85 |
| Max Drawdown (10Y) | −22% | −20% | −34% |

Over 10 years, USMV has nearly matched VOO on a risk-adjusted basis (Sharpe ratio 0.84 vs 0.85) while experiencing a maximum drawdown 12 percentage points shallower. SPLV has lagged on both absolute and risk-adjusted returns — the cost of excessive sector concentration in utilities and staples.

## The Case for a Permanent Defensive Allocation

Most investors buy low-volatility ETFs **after** a selloff has already happened — buying USMV in November 2022, after the S&P 500 had already fallen 19%, missing the protection they needed and then suffering the underperformance in 2023-2024. This is the worst of both worlds: you pay for the defense without getting the protection, then you suffer the underperformance without having enjoyed the bull market.

The better approach: **permanent defensive allocation**. Allocate 10-20% of your equity portfolio to low-volatility ETFs as a structural position, not a tactical trade. Rebalance annually. Accept that it will underperform in bull markets. The payoff comes during bear markets — not just in returns saved, but in behavior preserved. The investor who sells at the bottom because they can't handle the drawdown loses far more than the investor who holds a 15% low-vol allocation and underperforms by 3% in a good year.

## The Bottom Line

Low-volatility ETFs are not a way to beat the market. They are a way to **stay in the market**. The biggest risk to long-term returns is not a 20% drawdown — it's selling at the bottom of one. USMV and SPLV reduce drawdowns enough that you're less likely to make the catastrophic behavioral error that destroys a decade of compounding.

For most investors, **USMV (0.15%) is the better low-vol vehicle**. Its optimization approach captures more upside than SPLV while still providing meaningful downside protection. Over 10 years, it has delivered superior risk-adjusted returns with half the drawdown of the S&P 500. SPLV (0.25%) provides more protection but at a higher long-term cost — it's a purer defensive play for investors who prioritize capital preservation over total return.

The key is permanence. Don't buy low-vol after a crash. Own it before one. The investor who holds a 15% USMV position through a bull market, underperforming by a few percentage points, and then watches it lose 8% instead of 19% in the next bear market will be grateful — not just for the money saved, but for the sleep preserved.
