# ETF Tax Efficiency: How US, HK, and Mainland China Compare for Investors

**By ETF Bridge Research** | July 1, 2026 | 10 min read

---

A 3.5% dividend yield sounds great — until you lose 30% of it to tax. A 25% capital gain feels good — until half of it goes to the taxman.

The ETF wrapper itself is tax-efficient — low turnover, in-kind redemptions, minimal distributions. But the tax regime of the **jurisdiction where the ETF is domiciled** can quietly consume returns that compound into five-figure differences over a career of investing.

This article compares the tax treatment of ETFs in the three most relevant jurisdictions for global investors: **the United States, Hong Kong, and Mainland China**. We'll focus on what matters most — dividend withholding, capital gains, and estate tax — and show which jurisdiction is tax-optimal for which investor.

---

## The Tax Landscape at a Glance

| Tax | US-Domiciled ETF (VOO) | HK-Domiciled ETF (2800.HK) | China Onshore ETF (510300) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dividend Withholding (foreign investor)** | 30% (non-treaty) / 15% (treaty, e.g., China) / 0% (US resident) | 0% (no HK dividend tax) | 10% (standard for foreign investors via Stock Connect) |
| **Capital Gains Tax (foreign investor)** | 0% (non-US persons not taxed on US capital gains from securities) | 0% (HK does not tax capital gains) | 0% (currently exempt for foreign investors) |
| **Estate Tax** | Yes — up to 40% on US-situs assets above $60,000 for non-US persons | No (HK abolished estate tax in 2006) | No (China has no estate tax) |
| **Tax-Advantaged Accounts** | ✅ IRA, 401(k), HSA — tax-deferred or tax-free growth | ⚠️ Limited HK pension schemes (MPF) | ⚠️ Limited to domestic investors |

---

## US-Domiciled ETFs: Tax-Efficient for Americans, Brutal for Non-Treaty Foreigners

### For US Residents

US-domiciled ETFs like VOO, IVV, and QQQ have the best tax treatment in the world **for US taxpayers**:

| Account Type | Dividend Tax | Capital Gains Tax | Annual Tax Drag (VOO, ~1.3% yield) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Taxable brokerage** | 0–20% (qualified dividends) | 0–20% (long-term) | 0–0.26% (on yield) |
| **Traditional IRA / 401(k)** | 0% (tax-deferred — taxed as income on withdrawal) | 0% | 0% (until withdrawal) |
| **Roth IRA** | 0% | 0% | **0% — completely tax-free** |

In a Roth IRA, a US investor pays **zero tax on dividends and zero tax on gains — forever.** This is the closest thing to a tax-free lunch in investing.

Additionally, US ETFs benefit from **in-kind creation/redemption**, which allows them to avoid distributing capital gains entirely. VOO has never distributed a capital gain in its history. The tax efficiency of the ETF structure is a genuine structural advantage, separate from the tax rate.

### For Non-US Investors

The picture flips dramatically for foreigners holding US ETFs:

| Investor Country | Dividend Withholding Rate | Estate Tax Exposure |
|---|---|---|
| **China** (US treaty) | 15% | Yes (above $60K) |
| **Hong Kong** (no treaty) | 30% | Yes |
| **Singapore** (no treaty) | 30% | Yes |
| **UK** (US treaty) | 15% | Yes |
| **Ireland / UCITS ETFs** (alternative) | 15% (Ireland-US treaty, fund level) | No (Irish-domiciled) |

> A Hong Kong investor holding VOO loses **30% of every dividend** to US withholding tax. On VOO's ~1.3% yield, that's 0.39%/year — **13× the fund's expense ratio.**

This is why European and Asian investors often buy **UCITS ETFs domiciled in Ireland** instead of US ETFs. An Irish-domiciled S&P 500 ETF pays 15% dividend withholding to the US (at the fund level, via the Ireland-US treaty), then distributes the remaining 85% to the investor with no further withholding. The effective tax rate is 15% on the yield, vs. 30% for holding VOO directly.

## Hong Kong-Domiciled ETFs: The Tax-Efficiency Champion

Hong Kong has arguably the most investor-friendly tax regime for ETFs among the three jurisdictions:

| Tax | Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Dividend Withholding** | **0%** | HK does not tax dividends — for HK companies or ETFs |
| **Capital Gains Tax** | **0%** | HK simply does not have a capital gains tax — on anything |
| **Stamp Duty** | 0.10% (buy) + 0.10% (sell) = 0.20% round-trip | Recently halved from 0.26%. One-time trading cost, not annual. |
| **Estate Tax** | **0%** | Abolished in 2006 |

For a buy-and-hold investor, the annual tax drag on 2800.HK (Tracker Fund of Hong Kong, 0.07% ER) is effectively **0.07%** — just the expense ratio. There is no second layer of dividend tax, no capital gains tax on sale, and no estate tax risk.

### The H-Share Tax Layer

The one caveat: when a HK-domiciled ETF holds H-shares of mainland Chinese companies (as 2800.HK does), those companies' dividends are subject to **10% Chinese withholding tax at the source**. The ETF receives the dividend net of that 10%, and you receive it net of 0% further HK tax.

| Dividend Source | Withholding Before ETF Receives | Further HK Withholding to You | Total Tax |
|---|---|---|---|
| **HK company** (HSBC, AIA) | 0% | 0% | 0% |
| **H-share** (ICBC, CCB, PetroChina) | 10% (China withholding) | 0% | 10% |
| **Red chip** (China Mobile, CNOOC) | 10% (China withholding) | 0% | 10% |

Since the HSI is roughly 50% HK companies and 50% mainland Chinese, the blended tax drag on dividends from 2800.HK is approximately **5% of the yield** — or 0.15% of NAV (at 3% yield). Invisible in the expense ratio, but real.

## China Onshore ETFs: The 10% Standard

China onshore ETFs (listed on Shanghai/Shenzhen) have a consistent 10% dividend withholding for foreign investors via Stock Connect:

| Tax | Rate for Foreign Investors (Stock Connect) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Dividend Withholding** | 10% | Applied at source |
| **Capital Gains Tax** | 0% | Currently exempt (temporary — has been extended repeatedly) |
| **Stamp Duty** | 0.05% (sell only) | Halved from 0.10% in August 2023 |
| **Estate Tax** | 0% | China has no estate tax |

The 10% dividend withholding is straightforward — lower than the US non-treaty rate (30%), higher than HK's rate on HK company dividends (0%). On a CSI 300 ETF yielding 2.7%, the annual tax drag is **0.27%** — roughly 1.3× the fund's expense ratio.

### The Capital Gains Exemption Risk

China's capital gains tax exemption for foreign investors investing via Stock Connect is **temporary** — it has been extended but not made permanent. If it were revoked and a 10% rate imposed, a global investor selling a $100,000 position in 510300 with a $30,000 gain would owe roughly $3,000 in Chinese capital gains tax. This is a tail risk worth tracking, especially for large positions.

## Cross-Jurisdiction Cost Comparison

Let's put it all together. Assume a global investor holding $100,000 in a broad-market ETF, annual gross return of 7% (with capital gains of ~5.7% and dividends of ~1.3%–3.0%), held for 20 years:

| Scenario | ETF | ER | Div Tax | Cap Gains Tax | Est. Total Annual Tax Drag | 20-Year Net Value |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **US resident, taxable** | VOO | 0.03% | 0.20% (qualified div) | 0% (deferred) | ~0.23% | ~$371,000 |
| **US resident, Roth IRA** | VOO | 0.03% | 0% | 0% | **~0.03%** | **~$386,000** |
| **HK resident, holding US ETF** | VOO | 0.03% | 0.39% (30% on 1.3% yield) | 0% (no US cap gains for non-US) | ~0.42% | ~$357,000 |
| **HK resident, holding HK ETF** | 2800.HK | 0.07% | ~0.15% (blended) | 0% | **~0.22%** | **~$371,000** |
| **Singapore resident, US ETF** | VOO | 0.03% | 0.39% | 0% + estate tax risk | ~0.42% + estate | ~$357,000 |
| **Any foreigner, Irish UCITS** | CSPX | 0.07% | 0.20% (15% on 1.3%) | 0% | **~0.27%** | **~$366,000** |
| **China resident, onshore ETF** | 510300 | 0.20% | 0.27% (10% on 2.7%) | 0% (currently) | ~0.47% | ~$354,000 |

> **The 20-year tax gap between the best and worst scenarios is roughly $32,000 — 8.3% of the final portfolio value.** Tax optimization isn't theoretical. It's the single largest cost you can control.

## The Treaty Arbitrage

If you're a non-US investor, one of the highest-ROI things you can do is check your country's tax treaty status with the US:

| Treaty Status | Dividend Withholding | What to Do |
|---|---|---|
| **No treaty** (HK, Singapore, Brazil, most EM) | 30% | Avoid US-domiciled ETFs. Use UCITS (Ireland) or local alternatives. |
| **15% treaty** (China, UK, most of Europe, Japan, Australia) | 15% | US ETFs are acceptable but still more expensive than Irish UCITS (15% at fund level, 0% to you) |
| **Closer treaty or specific exemption** | Varies | Check. A few countries have unique arrangements. |

> **The Ireland workaround:** Irish UCITS ETFs (ticker: CSPX for S&P 500) are taxed at 15% US withholding at the fund level via the Ireland-US treaty, and pay zero further dividend withholding to you — regardless of your country of residence. For non-US investors, CSPX (0.07% ER) has a lower total tax drag than VOO (0.03% ER + 30% withholding).

## Key Takeaways

1. **For US residents, US ETFs in tax-advantaged accounts are the gold standard.** A Roth IRA holding VOO pays zero tax on dividends and zero on gains — effectively a 0.03% total annual cost.

2. **For non-US, non-treaty investors (HK, Singapore), avoid holding US ETFs.** The 30% dividend withholding tax is punitive. Use HK-domiciled ETFs (2800.HK at 0.07% ER) or Irish UCITS ETFs (CSPX at 0.07%) instead.

3. **Hong Kong has the most investor-friendly tax regime** — 0% dividend tax, 0% capital gains tax, 0% estate tax. The only friction is a small stamp duty on trading.

4. **China's 10% dividend withholding is reasonable** — but the temporary nature of the capital gains exemption is a tail risk to monitor.

5. **The estate tax exposure on US ETFs (>$60K) is a hidden risk for non-US investors.** It can take up to 40% of US-situs assets at death. This alone is reason enough for many non-US investors to avoid US-domiciled ETFs entirely.

6. **Tax optimization is the highest-ROI activity in ETF selection.** The tax gap between the best and worst channel can exceed the expense ratio gap by 10–20×.
